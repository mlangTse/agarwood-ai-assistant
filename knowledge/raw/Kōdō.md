An incense burner ( 香炉, kōro ) used for an ayamekō 菖蒲香 game, a part of kōdō 这是一种用于“菖蒲香”游戏的香炉，属于“香道”的一部分。

***Kōdō*** (香道, "Way of Fragrance") is the art of appreciating [Japanese incense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_incense "Japanese incense"), and involves using incense within a structure of codified conduct. *Kōdō* includes all aspects of the incense process, from the tools (香道具, *kōdōgu*), to activities such as the incense-comparing games *kumikō* (組香) and *genjikō* (源氏香).[^1] *Kōdō* is counted as one of the three classical Japanese arts of refinement, along with *[ikebana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ikebana "Ikebana")* for flower arrangement, and *[chadō](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chad%C5%8D "Chadō")* for tea and the tea ceremony.[^2]  
香道，即“香之艺术”，是一种欣赏日本香料的方式。它要求人们在特定的礼仪规范下进行香料的运用。香道涵盖了香料的制作全过程，从所需的工具到各种与香相关的活动，比如比较香味的游戏“组香”和“源氏香”等。 [^1] 香道被视为日本三大古典艺术形式之一，其他两个分别是花艺和茶道。 [^2]

## Etymology 词源学

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Kodo.svg/120px-Kodo.svg.png)

Kanji characters for Kōdō 汉字表示“功夫”

The word 香 *kō* is written with the Chinese [Kangxi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kangxi_radical "Kangxi radical") [radical 186](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_186 "Radical 186"), which is composed of nine strokes (although it can be expanded up to 18 strokes 馫). Translated, it means "fragrance"; however, in this context, it may also be translated as "incense".  
“香”这个词用中文的康熙字典部首 186 来表示，这个部首由九划组成（虽然实际上可以扩展到 18 划）。翻译成英文后，意为“香气”；不过在这一语境中，它也可能被理解为“香膏”。

The word 道 *dō* (written with the same character as Chinese *tao* / *dao*) means "way", both literally (street) and metaphorically (a stream of life experience). The suffix -道 generally denotes, in the broadest sense, the totality of a movement as endeavor, tradition, practice and ethos.  
“道”这个词与中文中的“道”或“道”字形似，其含义为“道路”。从字面上看，它指的是实际的道路或街道；而从比喻意义上讲，它则代表人生经历中的一条路径或方向。通常来说，后缀“-道”在广义上指的是某种行为、传统、实践或精神的完整体现。

In the search for a suitable term, translations of such words into English sometimes focus on a narrower aspect of the original term. One common translation in context is "ceremony", which entails the process of preparation and smelling in general, but not a specific instance. In some instances, it functions similarly to the English suffix *\-ism*, and as in the case of [tea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_tea_ceremony "Japanese tea ceremony") (*chadō/sadō* 茶道), one sees *teaism* in early efforts to illustrate *sadō* in English by focusing on its philosophy and ethos.  
在寻找合适的词汇时，这类词语的英文翻译有时会侧重于原始术语的某个特定方面。常见的翻译就是“ceremony”，它指的是一般的准备和嗅闻过程，而不是特指某个具体实例。在某些情况下，这个词汇的用法类似于英语后缀“-ism”。例如，在描述茶道时，人们会尝试用“teaism”来描述茶道的精神和理念。

Conversely, the English phrase *the way of X* has become more [productive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Productivity_\(linguistics\) "Productivity (linguistics)").  
相反，英语表达“the way of X”变得更加实用了。

## History 历史

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Ch5_wakamurasaki.jpg/250px-Ch5_wakamurasaki.jpg)

Fragrant scent played an important role at court during the Heian period (image from The Tale of Genji by Tosa Mitsuoki, 1617–1691) 在平安时代，芳香的气味在宫廷中扮演了重要角色（图片来自土佐光喜所著的《源氏物语》，1617–1691 年）。

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Japanese_-_Incense_Burner_%28%22Koro%22%29_with_Peonies_-_Walters_491755.jpg/250px-Japanese_-_Incense_Burner_%28%22Koro%22%29_with_Peonies_-_Walters_491755.jpg)

Incense burner ( kōro ) with peonies, Hirado ware, circa 1800 from Edo 带有牡丹图案的香炉，平治原陶瓷制品，约 1800 年，来自江户时期

According to legend, [agarwood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agarwood "Agarwood") (aloeswood) first came to Japan when a log of incense wood drifted ashore on [Awaji island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Awaji_island "Awaji island") in the third year of [Empress Suiko](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empress_Suiko "Empress Suiko") 's reign (595 CE). People who found the incense wood noticed that the wood smelled pleasant when they put it near a fire. Then they presented the wood to local officials.  
根据传说，阿古木（沉香木）最初传入日本是在圣哲天皇统治的第三年（公元 595 年），当时一根沉香木被冲到了淡路岛的海岸上。发现这根沉香木的人注意到，当把沉香木放在火旁时，会散发出令人愉悦的香气。于是他们便将这根沉香木献给了当地的官员。

Japan was the eastern end of the [Silk Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Road "Silk Road"). Incense was brought from China over Korea and developed over 1000 years. The history starts in the 6th century CE when [Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism") arrived during the [Asuka period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asuka_period "Asuka period"). Agarwood is known to have come along with the supplies to build a temple in 538 CE. A ritual known as *sonaekō* became established. *Kōboku,* fragrant wood combined with herbs and other aromatic substances, was burned to provide incense for religious purposes.[^3] The custom of burning incense was further developed and blossomed amongst the court nobility.[^4] Pastime of *takimono*, a powdered mixture of aromatic substances, developed.[^5] Fragrant scents played a vital role at court life during the [Heian period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heian_period "Heian period"), robes and even fans were perfumed and poems written about them, it also featured prominently in the epic *[The Tale of Genji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Tale_of_Genji "The Tale of Genji")* in the 11th century.  
日本是丝绸之路的东端起点。从中国通过朝鲜半岛传入的香木，经过千年的发展而逐渐成熟。这一传统的历史可以追溯到公元 6 世纪，当时佛教在飞鸟时代传入日本。据记载，在 538 年，用于建造寺庙的沉香也随之传入日本。随后，一种名为“烧香”的仪式逐渐形成。人们还会燃烧含有芳香植物的香木，以作为宗教活动的祭品。这种烧香的习俗在皇室贵族中进一步发展和完善。此外，将芳香植物磨碎制成的粉末也被用作装饰品。在平安时代，芳香物质在宫廷生活中发挥了重要作用——连衣服和扇子都会被喷洒香气，而关于这些香气的诗歌也出现在 11 世纪的《源氏物语》中。

*Samurai* warriors would prepare for battle by purifying their minds and bodies with the incense of *kōboku*. They also developed an appreciation for its fragrances. In the late [Muromachi period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muromachi_period "Muromachi period") in the 16th century, this aesthetic awareness would develop into the accomplishment known as *kōdō*, which is the art of enjoying the incense of smouldering *kōboku*. The present style of *kōdō* has largely retained the structure and manner of the Muromachi period (during this time, the [tea ceremony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_tea_ceremony "Japanese tea ceremony") and the *[ikebana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ikebana "Ikebana")* style of flower arrangement developed as well).  
武士们会通过使用香来净化身心，为战斗做好准备。他们还非常欣赏香的气味。在 16 世纪的室町时代末期，这种对香的美学鉴赏逐渐发展成一种名为“香道”的艺术形式，即欣赏燃烧着的香的气味。现在的香道风格在很大程度上保留了室町时代的风格和做法（在这一时期，茶道和插花艺术也得到了发展）。

Expertise concerning tiny pieces of exotic aromatic woods led in the 15th and 16th centuries to the creation of various games or contests. Some depended on the memorization of scents, some involved sequences that held clues to classic poems, and some were merely a matter of identifying matching aromas. Incense games became a "way" (*dō*), an [avocation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avocation "Avocation"). The way of incense eventually spread from elite circles to townsmen.  
在 15 世纪和 16 世纪期间，人们对各种稀有芳香木材的深入了解促成了各种游戏或竞赛的诞生。其中一些游戏依赖于对香气记忆的考验，一些则涉及与经典诗歌相关的线索，而另一些游戏则只是考验人们识别相似香气的能力。焚香游戏因此成为一种“生活方式”，一种消遣方式。这种焚香文化逐渐从精英阶层传播到普通民众之中。

During the [Tenshō era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensh%C5%8D_\(Momoyama_period\) "Tenshō (Momoyama period)") in the late 16th century, the master craftsmen Kōju was employed at the [Kyoto Imperial Palace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyoto_Imperial_Palace "Kyoto Imperial Palace") and practiced incense ceremony. The third Kōju served under [Toyotomi Hideyoshi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toyotomi_Hideyoshi "Toyotomi Hideyoshi"), the fourth under [Tokugawa Ieyasu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tokugawa_Ieyasu "Tokugawa Ieyasu"). The eighth Kōju Takae Jyuemon was known as a master of incense of particular note.  
在 16 世纪末的贞享时代，有一位名叫国寿的著名工匠在京都皇宫工作，负责主持香事仪式。第三位国寿是在丰臣秀吉时期任职的，第四位则是在德川家康时期。第八位国寿，名叫高栄寿右卫门，被誉为杰出的香事大师。

During this time the "Ten Virtues of Kō" (香の十徳, *kōnojūtoku*) were formulated, which is a traditional listing of the benefits derived from the proper and correct use of quality incense:[^6] [^7] [^8]  
在此期间，人们制定了《香之十德》（香の十徳，kōnojūtoku），这是一份关于正确使用优质香品所能带来的益处的传统清单： [^6] [^7] [^8]

1. 感格鬼神: Sharpens the senses  
	锤炼感官：提升感知能力
2. 清浄心身: Purifies the body and the spirit  
	净化身心：使身体和心灵得到净化。
3. 能払汚穢: Eliminates pollutants  
	能払汚穢：消除污染物
4. 能覚睡眠: Awakens the spirit  
	深度睡眠：唤醒灵魂
5. 静中成友: Heals loneliness 在宁静中结交朋友：治愈孤独感
6. 塵裏愉閑: Calms in turbulent times  
	在动荡的时代中寻求平静：在混乱的时光里找到安宁
7. 多而不厭: Is not unpleasant, even in abundance  
	多而不腻：即使数量众多，也不会让人感到厌烦。
8. 募而知足: Even in small amounts is sufficient  
	即使数量很少也足够了：哪怕只有一点点，也已经足够了。
9. 久蔵不朽: Does not break down after a very long time  
	久藏不朽：在很长时间后也不会分解。
10. 常用無障: A common use is not harmful  
	常用无障：这种用法并不具有破坏性。

Even today, there is a strong relationship and holistic approach in *kōdō* between fragrant scent, the senses, the human spirit, and nature. The spirituality and refined concentration that is central to *kōdō* places it on the same level as *kadō* and *chadō*.  
即便在今天，香道仍然强调香气、感官、人类精神与大自然之间的紧密关系以及整体性的思维方式。香道所追求的精神境界和高度专注的态度，使其与琴道和茶道处于同一层次。

Modern Japanese perfume brands are largely based on the tradition and aesthetics of kodo [^9].

## Material

In *kōdō*, a small piece of fragrant wood is heated on a small [Mica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mica "Mica") plate (*Gin-yo*), which is heated from below by a piece of charcoal that is surrounded by ash. All this is held in a small ceramic censer that can look like a cup. It is not usual for wood or incense sticks to be burned because that would create smoke; only the essential aromatic oils should be released from the wood through the heat below it.

Aloeswood, also known as [agarwood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agarwood "Agarwood") (沈香, *jinkō*), is produced in certain parts of southeast Asia such as Vietnam. The trees secrete an aromatic resin, which over time then turns into *kōboku* (香木). One particular grade of *kōboku* with a high oil content and superior fragrance is called *kyara* (伽羅).

Another important material is [sandalwood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sandalwood "Sandalwood") (白檀, *byakudan*), which originates primarily from India, Indonesia, southern China or other parts of southeast Asia. Sandalwood trees need around 60 years to produce their signature fragrance that can be deemed acceptable to be used for *kōdo*.

Other materials used are [cinnamon bark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cinnamon_bark "Cinnamon bark") (桂皮 keihi), [chebulic myrobalan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chebulic_myrobalan "Chebulic myrobalan") (诃子 kashi), [clove](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clove "Clove") (丁子 choji), [ginger lily](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hedychium "Hedychium") (sanna), [lavender](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lavender "Lavender"), [licorice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Licorice "Licorice") (甘草属　kanzō), [patchouli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patchouli "Patchouli") (廣藿香 kakkō), [spikenard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nardostachys_jatamansi "Nardostachys jatamansi") (匙葉甘鬆 kansho), [camomile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camomile "Camomile"), [rhubarb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhubarb "Rhubarb") (大黄 daioh), [safflower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Safflower "Safflower") (紅花 benibana), [star anise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_anise "Star anise") (大茴香 dai uikyo) and other [herbs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbs "Herbs"). [Shell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exoskeleton "Exoskeleton") fragrances (貝香 kaikō) and other animal-derived aromatic materials are also used.

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/RanJyaTai_Shosoin.JPG/250px-RanJyaTai_Shosoin.JPG)

Ranjyatai, a historical kyara wood dating to the 10th century CE, kept at the Shōsōin treasury in Nara

Raw materials such as agarwood are becoming increasingly rare due to the depletion of the wild resource. This has made prime material very expensive. For example, the cost of lower grade *kyara* is about 20,000 yen per gram. Top quality *kyara* costs over 40,000 yen per gram, or many times the equivalent weight of gold (as of late 2012). Though it can only be warmed and used once for a formal ceremony, it can be stored for hundreds of years.

If the particular piece of incense wood has a history, the price can be even higher. The highest regarded wood, *Ranjyatai* (蘭奢待), dates back to at least the 10th century and is *kyara* wood from Laos or Vietnam, and was used by emperors and warlords for its fragrance. It is said to contain so much resin that it can be used many times over. The wood is kept at the [Shōsōin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sh%C5%8Ds%C5%8Din "Shōsōin") treasury in Nara, which is under the administration of the Imperial Household.

The high costs and difficulty in obtaining acceptable raw material is one of the reasons why *kōdō* is not as widely practiced or known compared to the art of flower arrangement or the tea ceremony.

One of the oldest traditional incense companies in Japan is [Baieido](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baieido "Baieido"), founded in 1657 with roots going back to the Muromachi period. Other traditional and still operating companies include [Kyukyodo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyukyodo "Kyukyodo") (1663, Kyoto) and [Shoyeido](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shoyeido "Shoyeido"), founded in 1705. [Nippon Kodo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nippon_Kodo "Nippon Kodo") is also a major supplier of incense material.

## Types of incense

[Sasaki Dōyō](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sasaki_D%C5%8Dy%C5%8D "Sasaki Dōyō") (1306–1373), who was regarded as a paragon of elegance and luxury and the quintessential military aristocrat during [Nanboku-chō period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanboku-ch%C5%8D_period "Nanboku-chō period"), owned many incense woods and named them.

*[Shōgun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dgun "Shōgun")* [Ashikaga Yoshimasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ashikaga_Yoshimasa "Ashikaga Yoshimasa") (1436–1490) himself appreciated precious scented woods and collected some or inherited them from Sasaki. In order to properly organise the large collection of incense wood, he appointed the experts of that time Sanjonishi Sanetaka, who became the founder of the Oie School, and Shino Soshin, the founder of the Shino School. They established a classifying system for agarwood called *rikkoku gomi*, which means "six countries, five scents".

| Name | Character | *rikkoku* (country) | *gomi* (scent) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| *kyara* | 伽羅 | [Vietnam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam "Vietnam") | bitter |
| *rakoku* | 羅国 | [Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thailand "Thailand") | sweet |
| *manaka* | 真那伽 | [Malacca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malacca "Malacca"), [Malaysia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaysia "Malaysia") | no scent |
| *manaban\** | 真南蛮 | unknown | salty |
| *sasora* | 佐曽羅 | [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India") | hot |
| *sumotara* / *sumontara* | 寸聞多羅 | [Sumatra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumatra "Sumatra") | sour |

\* *Manaban* comes from the word *nanban* which means "southern barbarian", and was brought to Japan by Portuguese traders with unknown origin.[^10]

## Incense utensils

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Japanese_-_Cabinet_for_the_Incense_Guessing_Game_-_Walters_67255_-_Interior.jpg/250px-Japanese_-_Cabinet_for_the_Incense_Guessing_Game_-_Walters_67255_-_Interior.jpg)

Kō-awase-dogu-dana cabinet for incense games (Gold lacquer, Edo period, 18th century)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/%E9%A6%99%E9%81%93_%281%29.JPG/250px-%E9%A6%99%E9%81%93_%281%29.JPG)

A set of incense utensils in a nagabon (長盆) tray. Top left to right: Honkōban (本香盤), takigara-ire (炷空入), hidōgu (火道具). Lower left to right: shinoori (志野折), two kōro (香炉), ginyō-bako (銀葉箱)

Incense utensils or equipment is called *kōdōgu* (香道具). A range of *kōdōgu* are available and different styles and motifs are used for different events and in different seasons. All the tools for incense ceremony are handled with exquisite care. They are scrupulously cleaned before and after each use and before storing. Much like the objects and tools used in the tea ceremony, these can be valued as high art.

The following are a few of the essential components:

- three-tiered container (重香箱, *jukōbako*), for the incense, new mica plates, and burned out incense with its used mica plate
- long tray (長盆, *nagabon*)
- censer (香炉, *kōrō*), also known as *te-kōro* (手香炉, "hand-held censer")
- Mica plates (銀葉, *gin-yo*), where the incense wood is placed to stop it from burning if it were to be placed directly on the ash
- Incense holder board (本香盤, *honkōban*), a small, wooden tablet with a flower-shaped mother-of-pearl fittings upon which the small incense pieces on mica plates are kept on top for display after use, normally 6 or 10 in number
- white ash ([*Trapa. Japonica*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_caltrop "Water caltrop")), but also red ash or other precious ash can be used
- Shino incense packet (志野折, *shinoori*), folded paper packet used to keep incense wood chips (themselves in their individual packets), used by the Shino School
- box (銀葉箱, *ginyō-bako*), silver box for containing the mica plates
- charcoal (炭團, *tadon*), a special, and nearly odourless, charcoal briquette

A small vase (香筯建, *kōji-tate*), also known as *koji-tate* (火箸立), keeps the fire utensils (火道具, *hidōgu*):

- metal tweezers or tongs (銀葉挾, *ginyo-basami*) for handling the square mica plates
- ebony chopsticks (香筯), for picking up pieces of incense wood
- small spatula (香匙), for transferring incense wood onto the mica plate
- metal chopsticks (火箸, *koji*), used to move the charcoal
- tamper (灰押, *hai-oshi*), an object shaped rather like a closed folding-fan, used to gently tamp and smooth the ashes in the censer into a cone around the burning charcoal
- feather brush (小羽, *ko-hane*), to clean and brush off any ashes
- answer sheet holder (鶯), for securing the sheet of paper with answers onto the tatami mat

Some other items can be included:

- small incense container (香合, *kōgō*), for keeping incense in, mainly as part of the tea ceremony, or in general
- ash container (炷空入, *takigara-ire*), where fresh ash is kept

Most of the utensils could be kept in a special cabinet (*dogu-dana*). Influential families would order elaborate and expensive cabinets made out precious woods and lacquer and goldwork.

## Components

The concept of *Kōdō* is made of two essential components: *Monkō* and *kumikō*.[^11]

### Monkō

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Genji_chapter_symbols_groupings_of_5_elements.svg/250px-Genji_chapter_symbols_groupings_of_5_elements.svg.png)

Genji-Kō symbols

The art of enjoying incense, with all its preparatory aspects, is called *monkō* (聞香), which translated means "listening to incense" (although the 聞 Kanji also means "to smell" in Chinese). The aim is to let the aroma of the material infuse the body and soul and "listen" to its essence in a [holistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holistic "Holistic") manner, as opposed to just reducing it to smelling.[^12] *Monkō* has been depicted in [Japanese art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_art "Japanese art"), with a well-known depiction by the artist [Shinsui Itō](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shinsui_It%C5%8D "Shinsui Itō") (1898–1972).[^13]

### Kumikō

Kumikō is the social activity that results from sharing Monkō.[^11] Participants sit near one another and take turns smelling incense from a [censer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Censer "Censer") as they pass it around the group. Participants comment on and make observations about the incense, and play games to guess the incense material. Genjikō is one such game, in which participants are to determine which of five prepared censers contain different scents, and which contain the same scent. Players' determinations (and the actual answers) are recorded using symbols in *kō no zu* ([香の図](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E9%A6%99%E3%81%AE%E5%9B%B3 "ja:香の図")).

The *kō no zu* for *Genjikō* is *Genjikō no zu* (源氏香の図). The geometric pattern of these are also used as *[mon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mon_\(emblem\) "Mon (emblem)")* (called as *Genjikō-mon* (源氏香紋)), for decoration in a number of other areas such as *[kimono](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kimono "Kimono")*, [Japanese lacquerware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_lacquerware "Japanese lacquerware") and [Japanese pottery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_pottery "Japanese pottery").
